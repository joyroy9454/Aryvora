Welcome

You ask AI a question. You get a mediocre answer. You try again. Still mediocre. You blame the tool.

But here’s the secret: the tool is only as good as the prompt you give it.

This book teaches you to write prompts that produce exactly what you need — whether you’re writing an essay, debugging code, analyzing data, or brainstorming business ideas. These are the same techniques used by AI engineers, researchers, and power users. Now they’re yours.

Let’s begin.

Chapter 1 — Why Prompts Matter

The $0.02 Difference

In 2024, a McKinsey study found that employees using structured prompt techniques completed tasks 40% faster than those using casual prompts. Same tools. Same models. Different prompts.

The difference between a vague answer and a brilliant one often comes down to two or three extra sentences in your prompt.

The Prompt Quality Spectrum

Think of prompts on a spectrum:

Level 1 — Vague: “Tell me about climate change.” Result: A generic encyclopedia summary.

Level 2 — Specific: “Explain the causes of climate change.” Result: Better, but still generic.

Level 3 — Structured: “Explain the top 5 causes of climate change, ranked by their contribution to global warming. For each cause, include one real-world example and one mitigation strategy.” Result: Actionable, specific, useful.

Level 4 — Expert: “You are a climate scientist writing for policy makers. Explain the top 5 causes of climate change, ranked by contribution to global warming. For each cause, include: (1) the mechanism in one sentence, (2) one real-world example with data, (3) one mitigation strategy, and (4) the economic cost of inaction. Use clear, non-technical language.” Result: Publication-quality output.

Most people operate at Level 1. This book takes you to Level 4.

The Compounding Effect

Better prompts don’t just give better answers — they compound over time. When you learn to prompt well:

  • You spend less time re-generating responses
  • You get more accurate information on the first try
  • You can tackle more complex problems
  • You develop a mental model for how AI thinks

Prompting is a skill. Skills improve with practice. This book gives you the framework.

Chapter 2 — The C.O.R.E. Framework

Four Elements That Fix 90% of Bad Prompts

C.O.R.E. stands for Context, Objective, Response format, Examples. Every great prompt contains these four elements.

C — Context

Tell the AI what background it needs.

Without context:

“Write a marketing email.”

With context:

“I’m a student running a small tutoring service for high school math. My audience is parents of 10th-grade students. Write a marketing email.”

Context transforms generic output into targeted, useful content.

O — Objective

State exactly what you want to achieve.

Without objective:

“Help me with my resume.”

With objective:

“Rewrite my resume summary to highlight my data science projects and make it compelling for entry-level analyst roles.”

The more specific your objective, the better the result.

R — Response Format

Tell the AI how to structure its answer.

Format options:

  • “Give me a bulleted list”
  • “Write in 3 paragraphs”
  • “Create a table comparing X and Y”
  • “Use the STAR method”
  • “Keep it under 200 words”

Example:

“List 5 study tips for exam preparation. For each tip, provide: the tip name, a one-sentence explanation, and a specific action item. Format as a numbered list.”

E — Examples

Show the AI what good output looks like.

Without examples:

“Write a product description for my study planner.”

With examples:

“Write a product description for my study planner in the style of these examples: Example 1: ‘The notebook that thinks like you do. 180 pages of dot-grid freedom for your best ideas.’ Example 2: ‘Stop forgetting. Start achieving. This planner turns your to-do list into a done list.’”

Examples are the single most powerful prompt technique. They eliminate ambiguity completely.

The C.O.R.E. Template

Use this template for any prompt:

Objective: [What you want to achieve]

Chapter 3 — Context Engineering

Why Context Is Everything

AI models have no memory of your conversation beyond the current chat window. They don’t know your project, your goals, or your preferences — unless you tell them.

Context engineering is the practice of giving AI exactly the background information it needs to produce relevant, personalized output.

The Context Stack

Think of context in layers:

Layer 1 — Role Context: Who should the AI be?

“You are an experienced academic writing tutor.”

Layer 2 — Task Context: What are you working on?

“I’m writing a 2,000-word research paper on renewable energy adoption in developing countries.”

Layer 3 — Constraint Context: What are the requirements?

“The paper must include at least 5 academic sources, follow APA format, and be written for an undergraduate audience.”

Layer 4 — Preference Context: What’s your style?

“I prefer concise, direct writing. Avoid jargon. Use active voice.”

The System Prompt Technique

For recurring tasks, create a “system prompt” — a context block you paste at the beginning of every conversation:

1
2
3
4
5

You are my personal study assistant. I'm a college student studying computer science. When I ask for help:
1. Always explain concepts step by step
2. Use code examples when relevant
3. Ask clarifying questions if my request is ambiguous
4. Suggest related topics I should study

Context Window Management

Long conversations lose context. Here’s how to manage it:

  1. Start fresh for new topics — don’t mix unrelated tasks
  2. Summarize periodically — “Here’s what we’ve discussed so far…”
  3. Reference earlier points — “Like we discussed in point 2 above…”
  4. Use the “recap” prompt — “Summarize our conversation so far and ask if I want to continue”
Pro Tip
Create a "context file" — a document with your role, preferences, and common tasks. Paste it at the start of every new chat. This alone will improve your AI output by 50%.

Chapter 4 — Role-Playing and Personas

The Persona Prompt

One of the most powerful techniques in prompt engineering is assigning the AI a specific role:

“You are a [ROLE]. [Describe what they would do].”

Examples:

For writing:

“You are a New York Times editor reviewing this article. Identify weak arguments, suggest improvements, and rate the overall quality from 1-10.”

For coding:

“You are a senior software engineer doing a code review. Check this code for bugs, security issues, and performance problems.”

For studying:

“You are a professor creating a final exam for Introduction to Psychology. Create 10 questions that test deep understanding, not memorization.”

For business:

“You are a venture capitalist evaluating this startup idea. What are the top 3 risks? What questions would you ask the founder?”

Why Personas Work

When you assign a role, the AI:

  • Adopts the vocabulary and perspective of that role
  • Applies domain-specific knowledge
  • Follows the decision-making patterns of that profession
  • Filters output through professional standards

Multi-Persona Prompts

For complex problems, use multiple personas:

“I’m deciding whether to pursue a master’s degree or start working. Give me advice from three perspectives: (1) a hiring manager in tech, (2) a professor who advises graduate students, and (3) a financial planner. Each perspective should be 100 words.”

The Devil’s Advocate

“I believe [your position]. You are a skilled debater. Give me the three strongest arguments against my position.”

This is invaluable for testing your own thinking before presentations, debates, or important decisions.

Chapter 5 — Chain-of-Thought Techniques

The Single Highest-Impact Technique

Research from Google Brain and OpenAI has shown that asking AI to “think step by step” improves accuracy by 40-80% on complex reasoning tasks.

This is the chain-of-thought technique, and it’s the most important prompt engineering skill you’ll learn.

How It Works

Without chain-of-thought:

“If a train travels 120 km in 2 hours, then slows down and travels 80 km in 2 more hours, what’s the average speed for the entire journey?” AI might get this wrong by averaging the two speeds.

With chain-of-thought:

“Solve this step by step: A train travels 120 km in 2 hours, then travels 80 km in 2 more hours. What’s the average speed for the entire journey? Show your work.”

Chain-of-Thought Templates

For analysis:

“Analyze [problem] step by step. First, identify the key factors. Then, evaluate each factor. Finally, synthesize your conclusions.”

For writing:

“Before writing, outline your approach. Then write each section. Finally, review for consistency.”

For coding:

“First, describe the algorithm in plain English. Then, identify the data structures needed. Then, write the code. Finally, explain how it works.”

For decision-making:

“List all options. For each option, list pros and cons. Then rank them by [criteria]. Finally, recommend the best option with reasoning.”

The “Explain Your Reasoning” Prompt

“Answer this question, then explain your reasoning step by step. If you’re unsure about any step, say so.”

This forces the AI to show its work, making it easier to catch errors and learn from the process.

Warning
Chain-of-thought improves accuracy but doesn't eliminate errors. Always verify AI's reasoning, especially for critical decisions, code, or academic work.

Chapter 6 — Iterative Refinement Loops

Great Answers Come from Conversation

The biggest mistake beginners make: they expect a perfect answer from a single prompt. Professional AI users know that the best outputs come from iterative refinement.

The Refinement Loop

  1. Draft — Get an initial response
  2. Evaluate — What’s missing? What’s wrong?
  3. Refine — Ask for specific improvements
  4. Repeat — Until you’re satisfied

Refinement Prompts

To improve quality:

“This is good, but make it more specific. Add concrete examples for each point.”

To change tone:

“Rewrite this in a more conversational tone, as if explaining to a friend.”

To add depth:

“Expand on point 3. I need more detail about the implementation.”

To reduce length:

“Condense this to half the length while keeping the key insights.”

To change format:

“Convert this into a step-by-step guide with numbered instructions.”

The “Yes, And…” Technique

Instead of starting over, build on what you have:

“Yes, keep the structure. And add a section about common mistakes. And include a checklist at the end.”

Knowing When to Stop

Refinement has diminishing returns. Stop when:

  • The output meets your needs
  • Further changes are cosmetic, not substantive
  • You’re spending more time prompting than the task is worth
Pro Tip
Save your best prompts. Create a personal prompt library — a document where you store prompts that worked well. Over time, this becomes your most valuable AI resource.

Chapter 7 — Subject-Specific Prompts

For Math and Science

Problem solving:

“Solve this problem step by step. After each step, explain why you’re doing it. Then verify your answer.”

Concept explanation:

“Explain [concept] as if I’m seeing it for the first time. Use an analogy, then give the formal definition, then show a worked example.”

For Coding

Debugging:

“Here’s my code: [CODE]. It should do [EXPECTED] but instead does [ACTUAL]. Find the bug, explain why it happens, and provide the fixed code.”

Learning:

“I want to learn [CONCEPT]. Explain it simply, show a code example, then give me a small exercise to practice.”

Code review:

“Review this code for: (1) bugs, (2) performance issues, (3) readability, (4) security vulnerabilities. Prioritize by severity.”

For Writing

Essay feedback:

“Here’s my essay: [ESSAY]. Evaluate: (1) thesis clarity, (2) argument strength, (3) evidence quality, (4) flow between paragraphs. Give specific suggestions, not general advice.”

Editing:

“Edit this for clarity and conciseness. Preserve my voice. Mark changes with [brackets] so I can review them.”

For Research

Literature review:

“I’m researching [TOPIC]. Find the 5 most important subtopics. For each, provide: key findings, major debates, and 2-3 seminal papers.”

Source evaluation:

“Evaluate this source: [PASTE SOURCE]. Rate its credibility, identify potential biases, and suggest what type of claims it can support.”

For Career

Resume tailoring:

“Here’s my resume: [RESUME]. Here’s the job description: [JD]. Rewrite my resume to match this role. Highlight relevant experience and use keywords from the JD.”

Interview prep:

“I’m interviewing for [ROLE] at [COMPANY]. Generate 10 likely interview questions. For each, provide a strong answer framework using the STAR method.”

Chapter 8 — The Master Prompt Library

20 Prompts You Can Use Today

1. The Explainer

“Explain [topic] to me like I’m 15. Use analogies and avoid jargon.”

2. The Summarizer

“Summarize this in 3 sentences: [TEXT]”

3. The Expander

“Take this idea and expand it into a detailed outline: [IDEA]”

4. The Critic

“What are the 3 biggest weaknesses in this argument: [ARGUMENT]”

5. The Comparator

“Compare [A] vs [B] across these dimensions: [DIMENSIONS]. Format as a table.”

6. The Simplifier

“Simplify this for a general audience: [COMPLEX TEXT]”

7. The Elaborator

“Elaborate on this point with examples and evidence: [POINT]”

8. The Devil’s Advocate

“Give me the strongest 3 arguments against: [POSITION]”

9. The Optimizer

“How can I improve this: [TEXT/CODE/PLAN]”

10. The Prioritizer

“Here are my tasks: [LIST]. Prioritize them by impact and urgency.”

11. The Teacher

“Create a lesson plan for teaching [TOPIC] to beginners.”

12. The Brainstormer

“Generate 10 creative ideas for [PROBLEM/PROJECT].”

13. The Editor

“Edit this for grammar, clarity, and flow. Don’t change the meaning: [TEXT]”

14. The Translator

“Translate this technical concept into business language: [CONCEPT]”

15. The Scenario Planner

“What are 3 best-case and 3 worst-case scenarios for [DECISION]?”

16. The Decision Matrix

“Help me decide between [OPTIONS]. Create a decision matrix with criteria and weights.”

17. The Email Writer

“Write a professional email that [GOAL]. Tone: [TONE].”

18. The Meeting Prep

“I have a meeting about [TOPIC]. Generate an agenda, key questions to ask, and potential objections to prepare for.”

19. The Learning Plan

“Create a 30-day learning plan for [SKILL]. Include daily tasks and resources.”

20. The Reflection

“Here’s what I accomplished this week: [LIST]. What patterns do you see? What should I focus on next week?”

ObjectiveWhat exactly do I want?“…about renewable energy adoption”

The Top 5 Techniques

  1. Chain-of-Thought: “Think step by step”
  2. Persona: “You are a [role]”
  3. Examples: “Like this: [example]”
  4. Constraints: “In 200 words, using bullet points”
  5. Iteration: “Good, now improve [specific aspect]”

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • ❌ Being vague (“Tell me about X”)
  • ❌ Asking multiple unrelated questions in one prompt
  • ❌ Not specifying the format you want
  • ❌ Accepting the first answer without refinement
  • ❌ Not providing examples when the task is complex

Workbook & Exercises

Exercise 1: C.O.R.E. Practice

Take this weak prompt and rewrite it using the C.O.R.E. framework:

“Write something about productivity.”

Write your improved version. Then compare it to this example:

Context: “I’m a college student taking 5 courses while working part-time.” Objective: “I need a daily productivity system that helps me study effectively in 2-hour blocks.” Response Format: “Give me a structured daily schedule with specific techniques for each block.” Examples: “Something like the Pomodoro technique but adapted for deep study sessions.”

Exercise 2: Chain-of-Thought

Ask AI to solve a problem without chain-of-thought. Then ask it again with “think step by step.” Compare the quality of both answers. Note the difference.

Exercise 3: Persona Experiment

Ask AI to give you career advice. Then ask again with: “You are a career counselor with 20 years of experience helping college graduates.” Compare the depth and specificity.

Exercise 4: Iterative Refinement

Pick a topic you’re studying. Ask AI to explain it. Then refine 3 times:

  1. “Make it simpler”
  2. “Add an example”
  3. “Turn it into a study guide”

Exercise 5: Build Your Prompt Library

Create a document with your 10 most-used prompts. For each one, note:

  • What it’s for
  • When to use it
  • Any variations you’ve discovered

Update this library every week. In a month, you’ll have a powerful personal resource.
